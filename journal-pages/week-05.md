# Designing with Data | Reflective Proposal 
## 1) Review and Reflect


During the course so far, my favourite experiment has been Week 2: Interactivity. I really enjoyed learning how to use the p5.js editor for the first time and experimenting with different shapes and simple interactions, such as buttons and sliders. I also enjoyed vibe coding something more creative, like my turntable project, because it allowed me to combine technical learning with playful experimentation. Another experiment I found meaningful was the interaction data portrait, as it was exciting to see my analogue data transformed into a digital form that could move and respond over time. 


Through these experiments, I became particularly interested in ideas about data humanism and the datafication of everyday life. I noticed that many of our activities involved turning personal experiences into data, whether that was movement, sound, or interaction. This made me reflect on how data can be used to represent emotional states, not just measurable physical events. Moving forward, I want to carry forward the idea that data visualisation can be expressive and empathetic. I am interested in exploring how design can communicate invisible or internal experiences, especially those related to mental health.

## 2) Thematic Focus and Data Source

For my design project I would like to focus on anxiety in young adults, as it is a common mental health issue, particularly among university students. It often involves feelings that are difficult to explain or measure, such as racing thoughts, restlessness, or physical tension. Because anxiety is largely invisible, it can be misunderstood or overlooked. A possible future scenario for this project is a world where emotional states are continuously tracked through wearable devices or self-reporting apps. In this scenario, individuals might monitor their stress levels in real time, similar to how people currently track steps or heart rate. 

The data for this project could be collected through a combination of self-recorded observations and simulated or live data. For example, I could record daily information about mood, stress levels, sleep, heart rate, or environmental triggers such as noise or workload. This data could be entered manually through a simple interface or collected using publicly available datasets related to stress or wellbeing. The interpretation of this data would focus on identifying rhythms, triggers, and fluctuations in anxiety levels. This approach relates to course ideas about data representation, particularly data humanism, because it aims to make emotional experiences visible in a way that feels relatable and understandable.

## 3) Visualisation & Impact

The final visualisation might take the form of an interactive digital artwork created in p5.js. For example, anxiety could be represented as moving shapes, pulses, or waves that grow, cluster, or become more chaotic as stress increases. When anxiety decreases, the visual system might slow down, spread out, or become calmer. The visualisation could update over time, allowing viewers to see how emotional states change throughout the day or week.

The provocation of this project is to question how emotional experiences can be translated into data, and whether visualising mental health could help people better understand themselves and others. Ultimately, I want the audience to develop empathy and awareness. Ideally, the visualisation will encourage viewers to reflect on their own mental wellbeing and recognise patterns in their daily lives. It may also prompt conversations about how technology can support mental health, while raising awareness of the complexities involved in turning emotions into data.
