## Final Artefact
### Happy Place | A Happiness Memory Archive
For the final outcome of my Designing with Data project, I created Happy Place | A Happiness Memory Archive, an interactive 3D data visualisation developed in Unreal Engine.
The project transforms memories from the HappyDB dataset into a navigable archive where visitors can explore real human experiences through interaction. Rather than displaying information through traditional graphs, charts, or statistics, the project represents memories as floating data objects within an immersive environment.

Each orb within the archive represents an individual memory. The visual properties of the orb communicate information from the dataset:
- Colour = Memory category
- Size = Age
- Interaction = Memory text

The archive contains memories grouped into four categories:
- Family
- Friends
- Achievement
- Exercise

Viewers move throughout the environment and approach memory orbs to reveal individual experiences submitted by participants from around the world.

## Final Project Statement
### Happy Place | A Happiness Memory Archive
Happy Place is an interactive data visualisation that transforms human memories into an explorable digital archive. The project uses data from HappyDB, a public dataset containing over 100,000 crowdsourced happy memories submitted by participants from around the world. Rather than representing this data through traditional charts, graphs, or statistics, the project visualises memories as floating orbs within an immersive three-dimensional environment developed in Unreal Engine. Each orb represents a real memory from the dataset and contains information about a participant's experience. The archive organises memories into four categories: Family, Friends, Achievement, and Exercise. Visual attributes communicate additional data: colour represents category, while orb size corresponds to the participant's age. Visitors can navigate the archive and interact with individual memories to reveal the personal stories behind the data.

The project is influenced by ideas of data humanism, which argue that data should be understood as a representation of human experiences rather than simply numerical information. While large datasets are often presented in ways that emphasise scale and abstraction, Happy Place seeks to reconnect data with the people and emotions behind it. By transforming memories into spatial objects that can be explored and discovered, the project encourages a more personal and reflective engagement with information.
The future scenario explored by the project imagines a world in which personal memories are increasingly collected, stored, and organised as data. In this context, the archive asks viewers to consider what happens when human experiences become digital records. What aspects of memory can be preserved through data, and what emotional qualities might be lost? Through interaction and exploration, Happy Place invites audiences to reflect on the relationship between memory, identity, and data, while demonstrating how data visualisation can move beyond statistics to communicate human stories.



## Final Reflection
Looking back on the project, I am pleased with how much the concept evolved from my initial proposal.
At the beginning of the semester I intended to focus on anxiety and mental health. While I remained interested in these themes, I struggled to identify a visualisation approach that felt interactive and engaging. Through experimentation and research, I became increasingly interested in memory as a form of data and the ways personal experiences can be represented through digital systems.

One of the biggest achievements of the project was successfully creating an interactive visualisation within Unreal Engine. Entering the project, I had very limited experience using Unreal and no experience creating interactive Blueprint systems. Learning the software required significant patience, experimentation, and troubleshooting. 

Many tasks took considerably longer than expected, particularly when developing interactions and learning the node-based Blueprint workflow.
The technical challenges were often frustrating, but they ultimately became one of the most valuable aspects of the project. By working through these difficulties, I developed skills that will be directly useful for future projects, particularly my capstone work, which also involves immersive and interactive environments.

Conceptually, I think the project successfully moved beyond a traditional understanding of data visualisation. Instead of presenting information through numerical representations, the archive encourages visitors to explore and experience the data spatially. The project demonstrates how data can represent human stories, relationships, and emotions rather than simply statistics.

If I were to continue developing the project, I would focus on increasing immersion and complexity. Potential improvements include animated orb movement, sound design, stronger visual effects, additional memory categories, and larger datasets. I would also be interested in exploring more complex relationships between memories to reveal patterns beyond simple categorisation.

Overall, this project challenged me both technically and conceptually. It pushed me to learn unfamiliar tools, think critically about how data can be represented, and develop an interactive experience rather than a conventional visualisation. While there are many aspects I would continue refining, I am proud of the final outcome and the skills I developed throughout the process.
